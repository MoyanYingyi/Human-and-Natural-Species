Introduction content

Invasive species are organisms that are intentionally or unintentionally introduced into environments outside their natural habitats. Once established, these species can spread rapidly because they often lack natural predators or competitors in their new ecosystems. Their expansion disrupts food chains, displaces native species, and causes severe ecological and economic losses. Human activities, including global trade, travel, and habitat alteration, have greatly accelerated the transcontinental migration of species, making biological invasion one of the leading drivers of biodiversity loss today.

Examples of Invasive Species

Species Origin Invaded Region Impact
Zebra Mussel Eastern Europe North America (Great Lakes) Clogs water pipes, disrupts aquatic ecosystems
Cane Toad Central & South America Australia Toxic to predators, rapidly spreads, kills native species
Asian Carp East Asia United States (Mississippi River Basin) Outcompetes native fish, alters food webs
Kudzu Plant Japan Southern United States Smothers trees, blocks sunlight, grows uncontrollably
Brown Tree Snake Australia & Papua New Guinea Guam Caused extinction of native birds and lizards
